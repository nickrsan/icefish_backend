Project Information

The McMurdo Oceanographic Observatory is the first and only real-time, multi-year oceanographic observing station in McMurdo Sound, Antarctica. It was installed by project B-195-M in November 2017 primarily in order to facilitate studies on the freezing avoidance strategies of Antarctic notothenioid fishes.

In addition to being the first formal effort at recording a long-term, archival quality seawater temperature, salinity and pressure (tides) dataset from McMurdo Sound, the Observatory is the only installation in Antarctica to stream a live, high-definition video stream and research-quality underwater audio recordings to the surface for ultimate worldwide dissemination.

Together, these unprecedented data streams are expected to provide unparalleled insight into short and long-term variation in the properties of McMurdo Sound seawater, visual documentation McMurdo Soundâ€™s unique physical and biological phenomena and, through underwater audio recordings of their vocalizations, to document the species of seals and whales that reside in and visit McMurdo Sound throughout the year.

Funding

The McMurdo Oceanographic Observatory was funded by National Science Foundation Polar Programs award #1644196 (2017 to 2020) entitled "The Oceanographic Drivers of the invasion of internal ice and its fate in Antarctic Notothenioid fishes" to Paul A. Cziko (P.I.) at the University of Oregon, Eugene and Arthur L. DeVries (Co-P.I.) at the University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign.

Our Team

Paul A. Cziko

Evolutionary biologist, tinkerer, diver and Principal Investigator of the project B-195-M. Paul studies the physiology and ecology of Antarctic fishes, especially as it relates to the evolution of their freezing avoidance strategies. He is Research Assistant Professor at the University of Oregon, Eugene, USA. {{ "paulcziko.net"|kiosk_url:location }}

Arthur L. DeVries

Art is the Co-Principal Investigator of the project and one of the most deployed Antarctic scientists ever. With his discovery of antifreeze proteins in Antarctic fishes in the 1970s, he opened an entirely new field of research into the physiology of cold adaptation and freezing avoidance. He is Professor Emeritus at the University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign, USA.

Henry Kaiser

Scientific diver, musician, videographer and field technician. Henry fills a number of important roles that every science group needs but rarely has. In twelve deployments to McMurdo Station and nearby field camps, Henry has supported numerous and varied science projects, both above and below the ice. {{ "henrykaiserguitar.com"|kiosk_url:location }}
